Mentoring

Trey Hunner / @treyhunner / Python & Django trainer

I'm Trey. I have taught Python to a lot of people. I've volunteered as a coach at over a dozen programming workshops. And for a living I do corporate training in Python and Django.

Focus on the task at hand

  • Avoid pointing out issues that aren't relevant
  • Don't nitpick over code style unless you're asked to
  • Focus on the immediate task, not everything that's wrong
  • Ask what is expected and run the code together to see what actually happens
- If you're helping debug code that doesn't do the right thing or doesn't look pretty, don't point out anything that is not immediately relevant - Don't discuss code style unless you're asked about code style - If a learner asks "what's wrong" and you see things that are wrong, don't immediately mention everything you see that could be wrong. - Ask them to tell you what they expected to see and show you what they're actually seeing and then step through the code aloud together. - Don't make assumptions and give out answers. Work through answers in steps by troubleshooting and walking through the behavior together.

Be deliberate and positive

  • Speak slowly and try to enunciate well
  • Be careful with words: don't say "obviously" or "it's easy"
  • Be on the same level: sit or squat instead of hovering
- Speak slowly and clearly - Be deliberate and cautious with your word choice. Don't say "obviously", "you just", or "it's easy" - Be deliberate about your composure. Try not to hover behind. Squat down next to learners, pull up a chair and sit side-by-side. - Try to lessen any perceptions that you are above your learners - Be positive and encouraging. Congratulate learners are their accomplishments. - Take everything step-by-step. If you don't see movement five steps forward that's okay. Baby steps are perfectly fine.

Supporter, not super hero

  • Dethrone yourself: be a helper more than an expert
  • Keep your learner in control: don't touch their keyboard!
  • Don't tell, show/do together
  • Walk through error messages together
  • Encourage looking it up: "I don't know, let's go to Google"
- You want to make sure your learner sees you less like an expert and more like a friendly helper. - Always have your learner in control: no touching the keyboard and no backseat driving. - Don't point out errors immediately. Walk through the error messages and step through the code with them. - Encourage looking things up.
